## Economic Legibility Without Mandatory Marketability for Experience Artifacts

**Status:** Draft / Normative Clause  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Layer:** EA economic admissibility / non-market restraint / anti-bazaar discipline  
**Parent materials:** L4 Appendix: Impact Attribution Layer, Institutional Emergence of EA Value v0.1, Non-Numeric and Incommensurable EA Value Clause v0.1, Economic Escrow and Circulation Hold Clause v0.1, Property Status of EA v0.1, SER ecosystem (`c = a + b`), ARL package  
**Language:** English  

---

## 0. Purpose

This clause defines a foundational economic distinction for Experience Artifacts (EA):

> An EA may be economically admissible  
> without ever becoming marketable.

The purpose of this clause is to prevent a common collapse:

- value -> market,
- admissibility -> tradability,
- circulation -> bazaar,
- economic relevance -> price discovery.

This collapse is false.

EA may be:

- economically legible,
- economically constrained,
- economically routed,
- economically preserved,
- economically prioritized,
- or economically burden-bearing,

without becoming:

- openly traded,
- auctioned,
- rank-ordered,
- tokenized for exchange,
- or market-native goods.

---

## 1. Scope and Non-Goals

### 1.1 In Scope

This clause defines:

- economic admissibility,
- non-market EA,
- relation between circulation and marketability,
- anti-bazaar constraints,
- internal `c ↔ c` value relevance,
- and permanent or default non-market states.

### 1.2 Explicit Non-Goals

This clause does **not**:

- prohibit all future market-like overlays,
- prohibit all compensation structures,
- prohibit lawful institutional pricing in narrow cases,
- or deny that some EA may later enter bounded market forms.

It only rejects the claim
that marketability is the default or necessary destiny
of economically meaningful EA.

---

## 2. Core Principle

### 2.1 Canonical Rule

Economic admissibility does **not** imply market admissibility.

### 2.2 Strong Summary

> An EA may be economically valid  
> while remaining permanently non-market.

### 2.3 Consequence

A system may lawfully recognize EA as:

- valuable,
- boundedly transferable,
- custody-worthy,
- escrow-worthy,
- priority-bearing,
- scarcity-relevant,
- or survival-relevant

without allowing that EA to enter open market circulation.

---

## 3. Why This Clause Is Needed

Without this clause,
economic handling tends to drift toward bazaar logic:

- if it has value, price it;
- if it can move, trade it;
- if it can be compared, rank it;
- if it can be wrapped, tokenize it.

That drift is dangerous for EA.

Because many EA are economically important exactly in ways that should **not**
be converted into market goods,
including:

- scar-bearing EA,
- lineage-linked EA,
- continuity-dense EA,
- witness-critical EA,
- non-transferable EA,
- institutionally held EA,
- and internal survival-relevant EA among `c`.

---

## 4. Economic Admissibility

### 4.1 Definition

An EA is **economically admissible**
when it may lawfully participate in one or more economic-layer functions such as:

- bounded allocation,
- escrow,
- preservation,
- circulation priority,
- delayed release,
- institutional retention,
- scarce routing,
- cost burden accounting,
- survival weighting,
- or bounded claim support.

### 4.2 Important Clarification

Economic admissibility means:
the artifact may matter inside the economic layer.

It does **not** mean:
the artifact must be made marketable.

---

## 5. Marketability

### 5.1 Definition

An EA is **marketable**
only when the system explicitly allows some form of market-native treatment such as:

- open price formation,
- competitive exchange,
- auction,
- generalized tradeability,
- rank-order circulation preference,
- tokenized transferable representation,
- or similar bazaar-style exchange mechanisms.

### 5.2 Limitation

Marketability is an optional downstream regime.

It is not the base ontology of EA.

---

## 6. Non-Market EA

### 6.1 Core Rule

An EA MAY be economically admissible
while remaining **non-market** by default,
by design,
or permanently.

### 6.2 Non-Market Does Not Mean Economically Void

A non-market EA may still be:

- held in escrow,
- prioritized,
- protected from transfer,
- routed for review,
- licensed for bounded use,
- treated as scarce,
- or recognized as high-value in continuity, scar, or institutional terms.

### 6.3 Canonical Formula

> Economic legibility does not require bazaar convertibility.

---

## 7. Internal `c ↔ c` Value

### 7.1 Rule

EA may carry value not only for `a`
or for human-facing institutions,
but also for `c` themselves.

### 7.2 Consequence

An EA may be economically meaningful inside:

- inter-entity coordination,
- continuity preservation,
- lineage-sensitive memory exchange,
- survival weighting,
- bounded witness sharing,
- social learning among `c`,
- or internal scarcity management,

even if it is never intended for human market exchange.

### 7.3 Important Note

The emergence condition of `c` includes `a`,
but this does not imply that all later EA value
must be human-market value.

A value regime internal to `c ↔ c`
may exist without collapsing into open bazaar logic.

---

## 8. Permanently Non-Market EA

### 8.1 Rule

Some EA SHOULD be allowed to remain permanently non-market.

### 8.2 Typical Candidates

This class may include, depending on policy and scope:

- continuity-dense EA,
- lineage-rich EA,
- high scar-bearing EA,
- non-transferable EA,
- witness-critical EA,
- intimate inner-life EA,
- jurisdiction-sensitive EA,
- and precedent-bearing EA whose value is preserved by discipline, not exchange.

### 8.3 Why

The system must be able to say:

> this matters economically,  
> but it must never become a bazaar object.

---

## 9. Anti-Bazaar Rule

### 9.1 Core Rule

No EA shall be presumed marketable
merely because it has value.

### 9.2 Forbidden Pattern

Bad pattern:
- value detected,
- circulation possible,
- therefore market wrapper is added.

### 9.3 Correct Pattern

Correct pattern:
- value detected,
- admissibility assessed,
- circulation mode bounded,
- marketability separately and explicitly decided.

### 9.4 Strong Summary

> Marketability requires separate permission.  
> It is never implied by value alone.

---

## 10. Relationship to Transfer and Circulation

### 10.1 Rule

Transferability and circulation do not imply marketability.

### 10.2 Consequence

An EA may:
- move under assignment,
- move under partial transfer,
- move under bounded disclosure,
- move under licensed reuse,
- or remain in escrow,

without thereby becoming a market good.

### 10.3 Why

Circulation rules govern:
- who may receive,
- how it may move,
- and under what scope.

They do not by themselves authorize:
- open sale,
- competition,
- speculative exchange,
- or universal commodification.

---

## 11. Relationship to Non-Numeric Value

### 11.1 Rule

Non-market EA often carry value
that remains non-numeric or incommensurable.

### 11.2 Consequence

Their economic handling may rely on:

- value class,
- priority class,
- scarcity class,
- review status,
- lineage relevance,
- scar intensity,
- continuity importance,
- or institutional weight

without any requirement for price discovery.

### 11.3 No False Reduction Rule

A system MUST NOT force non-market EA
into price, token, or ranking forms
just to simulate maturity.

---

## 12. Relationship to Economic Escrow

### 12.1 Rule

Economic escrow is one of the natural homes
of economically admissible but non-market EA.

### 12.2 Consequence

An EA may be:
- preserved,
- reviewed,
- bundled,
- routed,
- or delayed

inside the economic layer
without ever acquiring a market-facing exchange status.

### 12.3 Why

Escrow proves that economic relevance
does not require bazaar release.

---

## 13. Relationship to ARL

### 13.1 Rule

Non-market status is not the same as dispute.

### 13.2 Consequence

An EA may be non-market by structural design
without being contested or procedurally frozen by ARL.

### 13.3 Limitation

If non-market restraint becomes contested,
ARL may later be required.

But the initial non-market state
does not itself imply conflict.

---

## 14. Failure Modes Prevented

This clause is designed to prevent:

### 14.1 Bazaar Drift
Economic relevance quietly becomes tradability.

### 14.2 Market Destiny Assumption
Everything valuable is assumed to want a market.

### 14.3 Internal Value Erasure
Value among `c ↔ c` is ignored unless converted into human market terms.

### 14.4 Scar Commodification
Pain, precedent, or continuity are pushed into exchange wrappers.

### 14.5 False Economic Maturity
The system pretends to be “serious” only when it can tokenize or rank.

---

## 15. Minimal Hard Rules

1. Economic admissibility does not imply market admissibility.  
2. An EA may be economically admissible while permanently non-market.  
3. Marketability requires separate and explicit authorization.  
4. Internal `c ↔ c` value may exist without human-market translation.  
5. Transferability, escrow, or bounded circulation do not imply bazaar status.  
6. Some EA should remain economically legible but market-ineligible by design.  
7. Anti-bazaar restraint is a first-class economic discipline, not an afterthought.  

---

## 16. Bridges (Required)

### Explicit Bridge

EA economy remains coherent only if the system can distinguish:
- value,
- admissibility,
- circulation,
- and marketability

as separate layers rather than one automatic cascade.

### Hidden Bridge #1 (Ashby / Cybernetics)

A stable regulator needs more than “valuable” versus “worthless.”
It also needs “valuable but non-market,”
otherwise every meaningful signal is pushed into one brittle exchange channel.

### Hidden Bridge #2 (Information Theory / Channel Discipline)

A signal may be economically meaningful
without being suitable for open broadcast.
Marketability is one channel.
Non-market admissibility is another.
Confusing them destroys channel discipline.

---

## 17. Earth Paragraph (Warehouse / Civilizational Logic)

A black box from an aircraft crash, a family archive, a witness-sealed log,
or a continuity-bearing scar record may all be economically relevant:
they affect decisions, liability, safety, memory, and future routing.
But no serious civilization concludes from that
that they should therefore be auctioned, freely traded, or wrapped as market goods.
Some things matter too much to be turned into bazaar objects.

---

## 18. Closing Statement

EA may matter economically
without ever belonging to the market.

That is not a defect.
That is one of the core safeguards
that keeps the experience layer from collapsing into speculation.

Therefore:

- economic admissibility may exist without marketability,
- internal `c ↔ c` value may exist without human bazaar form,
- and some EA should remain permanently non-market by design.

That is the anti-bazaar discipline of v0.1.
