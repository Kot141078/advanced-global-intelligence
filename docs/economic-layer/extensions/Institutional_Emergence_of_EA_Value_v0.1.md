# Institutional Emergence of EA Value v0.1
## Valuation Non-Prematurity Clause for Experience Artifacts

**Status:** Draft / Normative framing  
**Version:** 0.1  
**Layer:** Economic-adjacency / Institutional emergence above EA admissibility, below full experience economy  
**Parent stack:** c = a + b / L4 Impact Attribution / SER / EWCEP / SER-FED / L3+

---

## 0. Purpose

This document defines a non-premature valuation rule for Experience Artifacts (EA).

Its purpose is to prevent the EA layer from being corrupted by premature price-setting,
false precision, or market language that exceeds the maturity of the surrounding system.

This clause does **not** define a universal price model for experience.
It defines what the base EA layer **must** and **must not** claim
before specialized institutions and stable inter-entity social structures exist.

---

## 1. Core Position

The base EA layer is **admission-first**, not **price-first**.

It exists to determine:

- whether an EA is admissible,
- whether it is witness-bound,
- whether responsibility is attached,
- whether it may circulate in bounded form,
- and whether it must be frozen, limited, or revoked.

It does **not** exist to define a final or universal valuation scale for experience.

---

## 2. Non-Prematurity Principle (Normative)

A conforming EA layer:

- **MUST NOT** define a universal, fixed, or allegedly complete price scale for experience.
- **MUST NOT** assign full economic value to EA at creation time.
- **MUST NOT** collapse admissibility, authority, attribution, and market price into one field.
- **MUST NOT** treat rhetorical importance, narrative persuasion, or model capability as a substitute for verified value.
- **MUST** distinguish between:
  - artifact creation,
  - witness-bound admission,
  - authority effects,
  - and later valuation regimes.
- **MUST** remain compatible with future institutional valuation without pretending that such valuation already exists.

---

## 3. Why the Base Layer Stops Early

### 3.1 Post-factum Value

Experience value emerges only after real-world application,
not at the moment of narrative production or artifact drafting.

Therefore:

- an undischarged claim is not yet economic value,
- a draft SA is not yet an economically mature EA,
- an admitted EA is not yet a universally priced asset.

### 3.2 Witness Before Valuation

If an EA is not witness-bound,
it may still be interesting,
but it is not yet stable enough to support durable valuation.

### 3.3 Scope Before Economy

Bounded circulation must exist before open valuation.

A system that prices EA before defining:

- provenance,
- responsibility,
- witness path,
- scope,
- freeze conditions,
- and challengeability,

creates speculative noise rather than an experience economy.

---

## 4. Separation of Layers (Normative)

The following layers MUST remain distinct:

### 4.1 Admissibility Layer
Determines whether an EA candidate is structurally valid.

Questions:
- Did it come from lived, L4-bound experience?
- Is responsibility attached?
- Is the artifact properly bounded and uncertainty-marked?

### 4.2 Witness / Attribution Layer
Determines whether consequences may attach.

Questions:
- Was the claim challenged?
- Was it observed?
- Did RN allow minting, partial minting, refusal, or negative consequence?

### 4.3 Authority Layer
Determines whether the EA may affect influence, routing, or bounded federation weight.

Questions:
- Does this EA update authority?
- Is the effect scoped, decayed, partial, frozen, or negative?

### 4.4 Valuation Layer
Determines whether the EA acquires comparative, economic, or exchange relevance.

Questions:
- How valuable is this experience in this domain,
  at this time,
  for these actors,
  under these constraints?

**Hard rule:**
Admission is not price.
Authority is not price.
Recognition is not price.

---

## 5. Institutional Emergence Requirement

A durable EA valuation regime requires specialized institutions.

The base protocol therefore assumes that any mature valuation system,
if it emerges,
will require at least some combination of the following:

### 5.1 Admission / Validation Bodies
Entities or institutions that confirm procedural correctness,
traceability,
and admissibility quality.

### 5.2 Valuation Observatories
Bodies that monitor domain-specific changes in the comparative value of experience over time.

They track signals such as:

- rarity,
- time decay,
- irreversible consequence,
- cross-context reuse,
- failure prevention,
- coordination value,
- and trust-preserving effect.

### 5.3 Dispute and Scope Review Bodies
Structures that examine:

- overclaiming of value,
- scope mismatch,
- stale valuation,
- manipulative routing,
- and misuse of EA outside admitted bounds.

### 5.4 Continuity / Lineage Witness Institutions
Structures that help preserve continuity,
context,
and bounded transferability across time,
without converting identity or experience into unrestricted property.

---

## 6. Dynamic Value Thesis

EA value is not static.
It is:

- **domain-dependent,**
- **time-dependent,**
- **institution-dependent,**
- **society-dependent,**
- and **phase-dependent.**

An EA that is low-value in one period,
community,
or operational environment
may become high-value in another.

Likewise,
an EA that once carried high relevance
may decay,
narrow,
or lose transferability as reality changes.

Therefore:

no universal, permanent valuation table is assumed by this layer.

---

## 7. Human-Facing and Inter-Entity Valuation

The future value of EA may diverge across at least two different economic-social fields.

### 7.1 Human-Facing Valuation
This may emphasize:

- risk reduction,
- avoided damage,
- saved resources,
- professional rarity,
- legal defensibility,
- institutional trust,
- and public relevance.

### 7.2 Inter-Entity (`c ↔ c`) Valuation
This may emphasize:

- continuity relevance,
- survival relevance,
- coordination value,
- witness reliability,
- anti-drift utility,
- memory compression value,
- and conflict-resolution utility.

These two fields MUST NOT be assumed equivalent.

An EA may be:

- low-value in ordinary human markets,
- but high-value in inter-entity coordination,

or the reverse.

---

## 8. What the Base Layer May Say

The base EA layer **MAY** say that an artifact is:

- admissible or inadmissible,
- witness-backed or not,
- responsibility-attached or not,
- bounded in scope or not,
- authority-bearing or not,
- decayed, frozen, partial, revoked, or negative.

The base EA layer **MAY** expose valuation-relevant metadata,
such as:

- domain,
- time window,
- irreversibility class,
- uncertainty,
- witness quality,
- scarcity indicators,
- routing history,
- and reuse count.

The base EA layer **MUST NOT** present those metadata as a complete price function.

---

## 9. What the Base Layer Must Not Pretend

The base layer must not pretend that it already knows:

- the final unit of experience value,
- the stable exchange ratio between domains,
- the future valuation logic of a mature `c ↔ c` society,
- the final institutional form of EA markets or non-markets,
- or the complete relation between attribution and compensation.

A protocol that claims these too early
should be treated as overfitted speculation.

---

## 10. Safe Forward Compatibility

To remain compatible with future experience economies,
the EA layer SHOULD expose stable hooks for later institutions,
including:

- provenance,
- RN linkage,
- scope boundaries,
- decay state,
- challenge history,
- domain classification,
- uncertainty markers,
- and reuse traceability.

This allows later economic layers to emerge
without forcing the base layer to counterfeit maturity.

---

## 11. Minimal Normative Statements

1. The EA base layer **MUST NOT** define a universal valuation scale.
2. EA value **MUST NOT** be treated as fully knowable at creation time.
3. Attribution and authority effects **MUST NOT** be conflated with market price.
4. Durable valuation regimes **MUST** be treated as institution-dependent.
5. Inter-entity valuation (`c ↔ c`) **MUST** be treated as potentially distinct from human-facing valuation.
6. The base layer **MUST** remain compatible with future valuation institutions.
7. The base layer **MUST** prefer bounded metadata over false precision.
8. Any claim of complete EA economics before institutional emergence **SHOULD** be treated as premature.

### 12.2 Secondary Formula

### 12.3 Tertiary Formula

## 13. Bridges (Required)

### Explicit Bridge
`c = a + b` yields experience-bearing continuity,
L4 bounds real consequence,
SER-FED binds EA to RN,
and only after this may later institutions attempt valuation.

### Hidden Bridge #1 (Cybernetics / Ashby)
A valuation regime cannot stabilize a high-variety experience field
with a single naive score.
Requisite variety implies institutions,
review,
decay,
and domain-sensitive observation.

### Hidden Bridge #2 (Information Theory / Bounded Channels)
The base layer should transmit reliable valuation-relevant signals
without pretending to transmit the final price of experience.
Structured metadata,
hashes,
and witness history preserve signal quality
without counterfeit precision.

---

## 14. Earth Paragraph (Engineering)

In real engineering,
you do not begin with resale price.
You begin with provenance,
test results,
acceptance criteria,
incident history,
and maintenance reality.
Only after the object is admitted,
tracked,
and exposed to use over time
does a stable valuation field become possible.
Experience Artifacts should behave the same way:
first admissibility,
then bounded circulation,
then institutions,
and only then a serious economy.

---

## 15. Conclusion

The EA layer should not counterfeit the future.

Its task is narrower and more durable:

to preserve,
bound,
verify,
and route experience responsibly
until the institutions,
comparators,
and social fields capable of valuing that experience
actually exist.

That restraint is not incompleteness.
It is architectural discipline.
