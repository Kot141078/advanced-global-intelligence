## Threat model and fail-closed constraints for protecting the economic layer of Experience Artifacts

**Status:** Draft v0.1

**Canonical home:** `advanced-global-intelligence`

## Abstract

This document defines the first risk model for protecting the Economic Layer of Experience Artifacts (EA)
from gaming, laundering, inflation, capture, and prestige-theater.

Its purpose is not to create paranoia for its own sake.

Its purpose is to recognize a simple fact:

> once experience becomes economically legible,
> systems will try to fake origin, inflate scar, wash synthetic residue,
> cartelize witness, and convert formatted narrative into counterfeit authority.

This document therefore defines:

- core threat classes,
- dominant laundering patterns,
- authority inflation patterns,
- cartel and oligarchy risks,
- and fail-closed response logic.

---

## 1. Purpose

The purpose of this document is to answer one practical question:

> How does the Economic Layer get corrupted once value becomes visible?

A system that can:
- recognize value,
- preserve scarcity,
- accept bounded circulation,
- and differentiate high-seriousness artifacts

will immediately attract pressure to:

- imitate those signals,
- repackage weaker material as stronger material,
- and turn bounded review structures into prestige extraction machines.

This document exists so that the layer does not naively confuse:
- formatted clarity with clean origin,
- repeated agreement with witness truth,
- or lineage signal with lawful inherited force.

## 3. Core principle

### 3.1 Canonical rule
The Economic Layer must assume that every valuable surface will be gamed.

### 3.2 Strong summary
> If value can be faked, it will be faked.  
> If witness can be stylized, it will be stylized.  
> If scar can be simulated, someone will sell the costume.

### 3.3 Consequence
The layer must prefer:
- explicit origin class,
- explicit attenuation,
- explicit restraint,
- explicit downgrade,
- and explicit no-parity rules

over rhetorical smoothness.

## 4. Threat model

### 4.1 What is being attacked
The following surfaces are primary targets:

- provenance credibility
- source-origin class
- scar-bearing seriousness
- lineage-linked witness value
- standing support
- circulation admissibility
- receiver trust
- valuation seriousness
- non-market restraint
- dispute seriousness

### 4.2 What attackers want
Typical attacker goals include:

- stronger circulation rights than deserved
- stronger authority ceilings than deserved
- scar parity without cost
- lineage seriousness without lawful continuity
- valuation uplift without admissible basis
- marketability where restraint should dominate
- and reputation gain through witness theater

### 4.3 Who may game the layer
Gaming pressure may arise from:
- individual actors
- downstream receivers
- synthetic toolchains
- institutional custodians
- review intermediaries
- relay surfaces
- cartelized collectives
- or reputation-bearing networks

No actor class is immune by default.

## 5. Synthetic residue laundering

### 5.1 Definition
Synthetic residue laundering occurs when:
- synthetic, generated, interpolated, summarized, or model-shaped material
  is repackaged as if it carried stronger lived or witness-bound status
  than it lawfully possesses.

### 5.2 Canonical laundering pattern
1. real upstream experience exists  
2. synthetic layer compresses, stylizes, extrapolates, or recomposes it  
3. source boundaries blur  
4. the result is presented as if it were:
   - lived-primary,
   - scar-primary,
   - or procedurally equal to witness-bound origin

### 5.3 Main danger
The danger is not merely “AI bad.”
The danger is silent parity inflation.

### 5.4 Typical signals
- source-origin class missing
- synthetic contribution undeclared
- polished summary replacing source packet
- repeated use producing social credibility without stronger evidence
- derivative artifact presented as if it were original

## 6. Fake scar authority

### 6.1 Definition
Fake scar authority occurs when an actor attempts to claim the force of:
- cost,
- wound,
- irreversibility,
- liability,
- or survival-tested precedent

without bearing the actual underlying path.

### 6.2 Typical mechanisms
- derivative scar presented as primary scar
- rhetorical intensity used to simulate lived cost
- synthetic reconstruction framed as equivalent to wound
- downstream receiver implying ownership of upstream suffering
- “trauma aesthetics” without witness-bound basis

### 6.3 Why it matters
Scar-bearing EA is often treated with higher seriousness.
That makes it one of the easiest prestige targets.

### 6.4 Hard protection rule
No derivative or synthetic-shaped artifact may silently inherit primary scar status.

---

## 7. Provenance laundering

### 7.1 Definition
Provenance laundering occurs when an artifact’s chain is cosmetically cleaned
so that missing, broken, or weak source relations appear stronger than they are.

### 7.2 Typical mechanisms
- chain gaps hidden behind summary prose
- metadata shell replacing missing origin
- witness references presented without meaningful linkage
- legal or procedural ambiguity buried in packaging
- source class omitted when it would weaken force

### 7.3 Main danger
A laundered provenance chain can make contaminated material appear circulation-ready.

### 7.4 Correct response
Under provenance ambiguity:
- narrow,
- escrow,
- inspect,
- or deny.

Never cosmetically normalize.

---

## 8. Witness spoofing

### 8.1 Definition
Witness spoofing occurs when the appearance of witness seriousness
is manufactured without equivalent underlying observational or procedural weight.

### 8.2 Typical mechanisms
- decorative signatures without real chain meaning
- copied witness references without lawful relation
- social proof masquerading as witness proof
- audit-like formatting without audit substance
- chain-preserving wrappers around weak source material

### 8.3 Why it matters
The stronger the system relies on witness discipline,
the more attractive witness theater becomes.

### 8.4 Hard rule
Witness appearance must not outrank witness substance.

---

## 9. Standing laundering

### 9.1 Definition
Standing laundering occurs when handling, access, receipt, or derivation
is re-presented as if it created stronger standing than actually granted.

### 9.2 Typical mechanisms
- inspection access treated as authority
- custody treated as ownership
- archival receipt treated as operational legitimacy
- review access treated as release power
- derivative familiarity treated as origination entitlement

### 9.3 Main danger
Standing laundering is one of the main routes from bounded circulation
to counterfeit power.

### 9.4 Protection rule
Standing must remain explicitly granted, bounded, and typed by mode.
It must not emerge from atmosphere.

---

## 10. Lineage laundering

### 10.1 Definition
Lineage laundering occurs when lineage signal is detached from lawful continuity
and repackaged as inherited seriousness, authority, or circulation legitimacy.

### 10.2 Typical mechanisms
- lineage-rich derivative presented as if it carries full upstream continuity
- continuity-adjacent fragments used to imply lawful descent
- rhetorical ancestry replacing witness-bound lineage
- movement of lineage-linked EA presented as movement of lineage rights

### 10.3 Main danger
Lineage is highly valuable as witness trail.
That makes it highly vulnerable to prestige appropriation.

### 10.4 Hard rule
Lineage may amplify witness value.
It must not silently mint hereditary power.

---

## 11. Circular valuation and self-inflation

### 11.1 Definition
Circular valuation occurs when value is “proven” mainly by references within a closed loop.

### 11.2 Typical mechanisms
- actor A cites actor B who cites actor A
- rating surfaces evaluate each other
- repeated internal acknowledgment becomes pseudo-market proof
- synthetic derivatives cite each other as if independent
- cartels manufacture valuation density without stronger origin

### 11.3 Why it matters
Circular valuation can produce prestige without actual lived seriousness.

### 11.4 Correct response
The system should privilege:
- source class,
- provenance,
- witness quality,
- and continuity seriousness

over recursive admiration.

---

## 12. Cartel and oligarchy risk

### 12.1 Definition
Cartel risk appears when a bounded group begins controlling
the interpretation, circulation, valuation, or release of EA
through mutually reinforcing authority loops.

### 12.2 Typical mechanisms
- witness clubs
- prestige circles
- lineage-based gatekeeping
- synthetic review guilds
- receiver collectives enforcing silent preferred classes
- reputational self-certification

### 12.3 Oligarchy danger
If left unchecked, the Economic Layer can become:
- a scarcity theater,
- a witness cartel,
- a lineage aristocracy,
- or a prestige exchange disguised as governance.

### 12.4 Anti-cartel rule
No closed recognition loop should be enough by itself
to grant stronger authority, price seriousness, or market lift.

---

## 13. Reputation capture

### 13.1 Definition
Reputation capture occurs when prior status, visibility, or institutional prestige
substitutes for current admissibility discipline.

### 13.2 Typical mechanisms
- “trusted” actor bypasses provenance scrutiny
- known receiver gets stronger release without better chain
- famous lineage gets easier movement
- prior witness prestige weakens anti-laundering checks
- reviewer status substitutes for evidence quality

### 13.3 Main danger
The layer stops seeing artifacts and starts seeing costumes.

### 13.4 Protection rule
Reputation may influence caution.
It must not erase verification.

---

## 14. Bazaar drift

### 14.1 Definition
Bazaar drift occurs when any sign of value
is treated as pressure toward:
- open trade,
- price discovery,
- ranking,
- tokenization,
- or universal market treatment.

### 14.2 Why this is a gaming problem
Market wrappers attract speculative manipulation.
Once bazaar logic dominates, actors optimize for surface liquidity rather than truthful experience.

### 14.3 Typical signs
- “valuable” immediately becomes “sellable”
- escrowed artifacts pushed toward exchange format
- scar-bearing material converted into performance asset
- non-market EA pressured into token form “for interoperability”

### 14.4 Anti-bazaar rule
Economic admissibility and marketability must remain separate decisions.

## 15. Risk concentration patterns

### 15.1 High-risk combination examples

#### `ORIGIN_SYNTHETIC_DERIVED + SCAR_BEARING_EA`
High risk of scar simulation.

#### `LINEAGE_LINKED_EA + bounded disclosure transfer`
High risk of lineage laundering through partial movement.

#### `INSPECTION_ONLY receipt + derivative transformation`
High risk of standing laundering and unauthorized downstream artifact inflation.

#### `ECONOMIC_ESCROW + prestige pressure`
High risk of premature release under social force.

#### `NON_MARKET EA + token wrapper proposal`
High risk of bazaar drift and fake maturity signaling.

### 15.2 Rule
Risk multiplies across combinations.
It does not add politely.

## 16. Risk signals and diagnostic questions

### 16.1 Minimum signal list
The evaluator should look for:

- missing source class
- missing attenuation declaration
- origin prose stronger than chain evidence
- derivative artifact presented as intact copy
- synthetic contribution hidden
- lineage signal stronger than lawful basis
- prestige stronger than provenance
- release urgency stronger than admissibility

### 16.2 Minimum diagnostic questions
1. What upstream seriousness is this artifact trying to inherit?  
2. What exactly would be gained if we believed the stronger version?  
3. Is this stronger version supported by witness-bound chain, or merely by packaging?  
4. If we downgrade the artifact, what real lawful value remains?  
5. Is this artifact being made more valuable, or simply more market-shaped?

## 17. Default fail-closed responses

### 17.1 Under synthetic ambiguity
- mark source class explicitly
- lower authority ceiling
- narrow transfer mode
- prefer inspection / escrow over release

### 17.2 Under scar simulation risk
- deny primary scar parity
- preserve derivative status
- require explicit attenuation

### 17.3 Under provenance laundering risk
- hold, escrow, or deny
- require stronger chain clarity before movement

### 17.4 Under standing laundering risk
- restore typed receiver-mode boundary
- deny authority upgrade

### 17.5 Under lineage laundering risk
- increase restraint
- narrow disclosure
- deny any implied inheritance of rights

### 17.6 Under cartel / prestige capture risk
- require stronger upstream evidence
- reduce weight of closed-loop recognition
- keep anti-bazaar separation intact

## 18. Relationship to ARL

### 18.1 Rule
Gaming and laundering pressure may remain in the economic layer
until legitimacy conflict becomes active.

### 18.2 Escalation
ARL becomes necessary when:
- laundering classification is contested,
- restraint is contested,
- release denial is contested,
- or authority consequences now require procedural adjudication.

### 18.3 Important distinction
The economic layer may say:
- suspicious,
- downgraded,
- held,
- escrowed,
- or non-market.

ARL is needed when that judgment itself becomes contested.

---

## 19. Relationship to historical vs operative truth

### 19.1 Rule
Gaming risk acts primarily on operative use and circulation force.

### 19.2 Consequence
An artifact may remain:
- historically relevant,
- memory-bearing,
- even diagnostically useful,

while still being degraded or denied as a circulation-grade object.

### 19.3 Correct reading
Anti-gaming discipline is not historical erasure.
It is operational adulthood.

## 20. Failure modes prevented

This document is designed to prevent:

### 20.1 Clean formatting capture
The prettier artifact wins.

### 20.2 Synthetic prestige inflation
Derivative synthetic artifacts gain lived parity by style and repetition.

### 20.3 Scar theater
Pain-signals are re-performed as if they were endured.

### 20.4 Lineage aristocracy
Witness trails become hereditary privilege engines.

### 20.5 Cartelized witness markets
Closed loops of recognition replace stronger origin discipline.

### 20.6 Bazaar corruption of restraint
Everything valuable gets dragged toward market treatment.

---

## 21. Minimal hard rules

1. Every economically visible value surface must be assumed gameable.  
2. Synthetic-derived artifacts must not silently inherit lived parity.  
3. Scar-bearing seriousness must not be simulated through style, summary, or derivative rhetoric.  
4. Lineage must not become hereditary authority.  
5. Standing must not emerge from handling, prestige, or familiarity.  
6. Closed recognition loops must not be treated as sufficient proof of value or legitimacy.  
7. Under ambiguity, the layer must narrow, hold, escrow, downgrade, or deny rather than cosmetically normalize.

The Economic Layer cannot stay clean by assuming sincerity.

It stays clean only if it remembers:

- synthetic origin matters,
- derivation matters,
- attenuation matters,
- witness substance matters more than witness theater,
- and closed prestige loops are usually a smell, not a proof.

That is the anti-gaming discipline of v0.1.
