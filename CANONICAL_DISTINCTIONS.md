# Canonical Distinctions

## Why this file exists

This file fixes a small set of recurring corpus-level confusions in one place.
It is short on purpose: citation surface, not essay surface.
Use `TERMINOLOGY_AND_ALIAS_POLICY.md` when the question is canonical wording or allowed alias discipline rather than one of the specific doctrinal confusions fixed below.

## AGI != monolithic super-model

- Often confused: AGI is read here as one all-capable model.
- Canonical distinction: AGI here means ecosystem framing for `c = a + b`, SER, `L4`, and adjacent layers.
- Why it matters operationally: it prevents governance, continuity, feasibility, and runtime from collapsing into one model claim.

## c != agent

- Often confused: `c` is treated as just another worker, tool, or model process.
- Canonical distinction: `c` is the continuity-bearing entity layer; agents are bounded runtime processes under it.
- Why it matters operationally: authority, stopping power, continuity, and review must stay at the `c` layer.

## c != generic chatbot

- Often confused: `c` is reduced to a conversational front-end.
- Canonical distinction: chat may be one interface, but `c` is defined by bounded accountable operation, not by chat format.
- Why it matters operationally: protocol, review, perimeter, and evidence cannot be inferred from conversational style.

## continuity != resemblance

- Often confused: continuity is replaced by similarity of tone, memory fragments, or output style.
- Canonical distinction: continuity is narrower than resemblance and depends on preserved operating identity conditions.
- Why it matters operationally: style match alone cannot justify privilege carry-over or identity claims.

## oracle != identity-bearing node

- Often confused: an oracle-like process is treated as if it carries entity continuity.
- Canonical distinction: oracle is stateless and not identity-bearing.
- Why it matters operationally: advisory or inference surfaces must not silently become continuity anchors.

## arbiter / review layer != owner of continuity

- Often confused: review or arbitration is treated as the place where identity resides.
- Canonical distinction: arbiter and review layers constrain, examine, and authorize; they do not own continuity.
- Why it matters operationally: review must remain accountable without becoming the hidden identity core.

## EA != LA

- Often confused: experience artifacts are treated as lineage artifacts.
- Canonical distinction: EA is not LA.
- Why it matters operationally: provenance, admissibility, transfer, inheritance, and continuity rules stay separable.

## historical truth != operative truth

- Often confused: archival or narrative truth is treated as the same thing as current operative state.
- Canonical distinction: historical truth and operative truth can both matter without being interchangeable.
- Why it matters operationally: current decisions must bind to active state, not just to historical description.

## L3 permission != L4 feasibility

- Often confused: if something is normatively allowed, it is assumed to be operationally possible.
- Canonical distinction: `L3` permission is not `L4` feasibility.
- Why it matters operationally: bounded power, hardware, trust, witnessability, and time can still block execution.

## witness trail != narrative claim

- Often confused: a persuasive story is treated as if it were a witness-backed trail.
- Canonical distinction: witness trail requires reviewable evidence; narrative claim does not.
- Why it matters operationally: promotion, accountability, and dispute handling need traceable records rather than rhetoric.

## local-first != anti-cloud ideology

- Often confused: local-first is read as a cultural rejection of cloud use.
- Canonical distinction: local-first is an operational priority for control, failure bounds, and reviewability, not a blanket ideology.
- Why it matters operationally: deployment choices stay subordinate to accountability and boundary discipline.

## clean-code skeleton != commercial product

- Often confused: the executable layer is treated as if it were a finished market product.
- Canonical distinction: `ester-clean-code` is an executable operational skeleton and runtime proof-of-possibility.
- Why it matters operationally: closure evidence and bounded implementation should not be misread as packaging, support, or product claims.

## narrative companion != normative core

- Often confused: the book is treated as if it replaces the corpus normative stack.
- Canonical distinction: the book is the human-facing narrative companion, not the normative core.
- Why it matters operationally: protocol meaning and narrative recognition stay connected without becoming the same layer.

## book evidence-of-perception-shift != protocol evidence

- Often confused: narrative recognition of a shift is treated as proof that a protocol claim is satisfied.
- Canonical distinction: the book can evidence perception shift; it does not constitute protocol evidence.
- Why it matters operationally: witness trails, feasibility claims, and normative closure still require protocol-grade evidence.
